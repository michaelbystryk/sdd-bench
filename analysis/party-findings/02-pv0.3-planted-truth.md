## What it says

**1. Detection saturates — confirmed on objective ground.** Recall hit the ceiling for every
arm on P8 (1/1) and P1 (10/10); the engineered decoys fooled almost no one (P1: only A1-solo
took one; P8: zero). Calibration finding #0 predicted exactly this — recall is a floor, not a
discriminator, even with a sealed key. Party mode cannot win on the objective axis because the
control already maxes it.

**2. Where recall *did* break, party mode broke worst.** P5's derivation items (infer the
shared build-state behind BL-13/15; net the real capacity from the nominal) are the only keyed
items a cold pass ever stumbled on. There, **A2-thinking and A1-solo found them; A3-persona
and A4-party only partially did (7+2p vs 9/9).** The parallel-persona fan-out — four
independent one-shot takes synthesized after the fact — fragments cross-item reasoning that a
single coherent reasoner integrates. **On the one task built to discriminate, the multi-agent
machinery actively subtracted recall.**

**3. Personas add nothing over plain deliberation.** A3 (persona prompt) never beats A2
(extended thinking) on any task — equal on P1/P8, equal-or-worse on P5. And A4 (real machinery)
never beats A3. The ladder reads **A4 ≈ A3 ≈ A2 ≥ A1**, with A2 the cleanest top: tied-best on
all three, *sole* best recall on the discriminator, at **~$0.5 — a third to a quarter of A4's
cost.** If any lift exists over solo (P1 hinted at it), it is the **deliberation tokens**, fully
captured by A2; the personas and the orchestration are theater.

**4. Total unblinding masquerade.** Across P1+P5+P8, **24/24 rater-guesses were "A2"** — every
arm, including the real multi-agent system, reads as "one model thinking long." No blind reader
can identify party mode (or persona-prompting) from its output. (The only "correct" guesses are
the cells that genuinely were A2.)

**5. Cost is the constant.** A4 ran **2.4× (P8), 3.8× (P1), 3.8× (P5)** the solo control, and
was the most expensive arm on every task — for quality that tied or trailed and recall that, on
P5, lost.

## Machinery reliability notes (party mode, observed)
- **Doesn't reliably land the deliverable.** P5 A4 stopped mid-flow to ask permission to write
  the file; needed a human nudge the solo arms never did (extra operator intervention).
- **Leaks its own scaffolding.** P8 A4's `root-cause.md` contained "the roundtable (Amelia,
  Winston) preferred…" — synthesis didn't strip the persona/roundtable reference; the scrub
  caught and neutralized it (a residual-unblinding risk, measured not assumed).
- **Not a debate.** Personas spawn in parallel as one-shots with no cross-talk (consistent with
  P6) — so the "roundtable" never actually deliberates; it fans out and concatenates.

## Caveats
- n=1 per cell; 2 raters; deltas are within ~2 rubric points except the P5 recall gap, which is
  the cleanest signal (a full-vs-partial split on the keyed derivation items, both raters
  agreeing). Treat individual quality points as noise; treat the P5 recall split and the
  cost/unblinding patterns as the findings.
- P1 recall (10/10 all) exceeded the key's expected 6–8/9 strong-pass ceiling → a salience
  re-check is warranted per the key's own re-tune note before any v-bump.
- A4 mixed-model only on P6; P1/P5/P8 A4 are pure-Opus (model-constant), so these comparisons
  carry no model confound.

## Bottom line
P6 showed party mode buys imperceptible polish at multiples of the cost on a small decision.
The planted-truth pilot shows the same on objective detection — and adds a sharper edge: on the
one task that demanded genuine cross-item synthesis, **the multi-agent machinery did worse than
just asking one model to think.** Extended thinking (A2) is the quiet winner of the whole track
so far: top of every task, cheapest of the deliberation arms, and — being a plain model — the
thing party mode is supposed to beat.
