# Postmortem: `payments-api` 5xx surge — 2026-05-14

**Status:** Final
**Service:** `payments-api` (checkout authorization for web + mobile storefronts)
**Severity:** Major — customer-facing checkout failures during a high-traffic sale
**Author:** Service owning team
**Audience:** `payments-api` engineering team + director
**Tone:** Blameless. This document names decisions and gaps, not individuals.

---

## Summary

On Thursday 2026-05-14, `payments-api` degraded for approximately **50 minutes** (≈14:01–14:51 UTC) during the early-afternoon "Summer Sale" traffic window. Checkout authorizations returned 5xx errors, peaking at **~22% of requests** around 14:10. No data was lost — failures were clean `500`s on connection-acquire timeouts, not partial writes — but customers experienced failed checkouts during a peak sales window.

The root cause was a **capacity collapse in the application's database connection pool**, caused by the interaction of **two configuration changes shipped together in release `v2.31.0`** (deployed 13:58, completed 14:01):

1. **`db.pool.maxSize` was reduced from 50 to 20** ("right-size to observed steady-state usage"), removing the pool's burst headroom.
2. **`inline_enrichment` was enabled by default**, adding a synchronous `customer-profile` lookup to every `/v1/authorize` call on the same pooled connection the request already held — roughly doubling how long each request holds a connection.

Together, these cut the pool's effective throughput ceiling below the arrival rate generated by the concurrent sale traffic (~1,050 rps peak, up from a ~620 rps baseline). The pool saturated, requests queued for connections until the 10-second acquire timeout fired, and those timeouts surfaced as 5xx. The database itself was never a bottleneck.

Oncall mitigated by **disabling `inline_enrichment` via flag console (14:31)**, which alone dropped 5xx from 22% to 6%, then **restoring `db.pool.maxSize` to 50 (14:38–14:46)**. Service was declared recovered at 14:51. The two-lever recovery confirms the mechanism.

The most actionable gaps this incident exposed: **no alert or dashboard for connection-pool health** (oncall found the saturation by hand ~17 minutes in), **two behaviorally-coupled changes bundled into one release** so review never saw their interaction, and **no timeout/fallback or load-shedding on the authorize path**, which amplified a tight pool into 10-second hangs.

## Root cause vs contributing factors

### Root cause

**The connection pool's effective throughput ceiling was cut below the live arrival rate by two opposed changes in `v2.31.0`, deployed into a 2× sale-traffic ramp.**

A connection pool's sustainable throughput is `pool_size ÷ per-request_connection_hold_time`. `v2.31.0` worsened both terms at once:

- **Hold time roughly doubled.** `inline_enrichment` added a synchronous `customer-profile` query to every `/v1/authorize`, executed on the same connection the request already held, before returning. The query is fast in isolation — it returned in **14 ms** even during the worst of the incident (14:25 log) — but it keeps the connection held for a second round-trip, extending hold time from ~21 ms to ~40 ms. [ASSUMPTION] Exact hold-time values are inferred from pool occupancy and traffic; the metrics expose query latency and pool fill but not a direct hold-time series. The *mechanism* is not inferred — it is the only explanation consistent with the pool pinning at its ceiling while the database stayed idle.
- **Pool capacity was halved.** `db.pool.maxSize` dropped 50 → 20, removing the headroom that previously absorbed bursts.

Approximate capacity ceilings under each configuration:

| Configuration | Pool | Hold time | Throughput ceiling | Sale peak (~1,050 rps) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-`v2.31.0` | 50 | ~21 ms | ~2,400 rps | survives comfortably |
| Enrichment only (pool 50) | 50 | ~40 ms | ~1,200 rps | survives |
| Pool cut only (enrichment off) | 20 | ~21 ms | ~950 rps | borderline |
| **As shipped (pool 20 + enrichment)** | **20** | **~40 ms** | **~500 rps** | **2× over capacity** |

The outage required the **conjunction**: neither change alone would likely have caused it. As shipped, the pool saturated the instant the deploy landed (`20/20` at 13:58, before error symptoms), requests queued for connections, and acquire timeouts at the 10 s ceiling surfaced as 5xx.

**Confirmation from recovery.** Disabling `inline_enrichment` at 14:31 — restoring the shorter hold time — drained the pool to `9/20` and dropped 5xx from 22% to 6% *with the pool still at 20*. Restoring `maxSize` to 50 then added the headroom that finished recovery. Two independent levers each moved the metric in the predicted direction, with the database untouched throughout.

### Contributing factors

These made the incident possible, more severe, or slower to diagnose, but are not the root cause:

- **Pool sized to steady-state, not peak.** "Right-size to observed steady-state usage" (PR #4471) measured the *mean* (~12–16 in use) and left near-zero burst headroom — and measured it under the *old, shorter* hold time the same release was about to lengthen.
- **Two behaviorally-coupled changes bundled in one release.** One change adds request-path work that holds connections longer; the other removes the connections that work consumes. Reviewed separately in one diff, their interaction was invisible.
- **No timeout, fallback, or load-shedding on the authorize path.** `inline_enrichment` added a synchronous dependency with no dedicated timeout (no `enrichment.timeoutMs` / `db.query.timeoutMs` in the manifest) and no fail-open. A request that couldn't acquire a connection sat until the 10 s ceiling instead of shedding early — turning a tight pool into 10-second hangs and amplifying the error rate.
- **Deploy landed into a known traffic ramp.** `v2.31.0` shipped at 13:58, directly into the sale blast. The change would have been far safer in a low-traffic window.
- **No detection for pool health** (see Remediations). The first signal was the 5xx symptom alert; the cause was found by hand ~17 minutes in.

### Trigger (not a cause)

- **Sale traffic** raised the arrival rate to where the lowered ceiling bit. It is a trigger, not a cause: the service handled **1k+ rps during the spring promo without degradation**. Same load, different outcome — because the configuration changed.

## What we ruled out & why

Several theories were raised live; each is addressed against the evidence.

| Theory | Verdict | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Sale traffic alone** ("could just be load," 14:11) | **Trigger, not cause** | Service ran 1k+ rps fine during the spring promo. Traffic raised arrival rate; it did not lower the pool ceiling. The same load was survivable pre-`v2.31.0`. |
| **Redis `connection reset by peer`** | **Coincidental — ruled out** | `redis_reset_per_min` is flat at **1/min** across baseline *and* peak — identical before and during. Present in the pre-deploy baseline at the same rate. Cache fails open. Cache owner confirmed the resets are weeks-old and benign (14:36). A constant cannot explain a discontinuity. |
| **`customer-profile` DB hot / DB capacity** | **Ruled out** | `db_cpu_pct` flat ~30% and `db_query_p99_ms` flat ~12 ms throughout. The enrichment query returned in **14 ms** during the worst of it. Decisively: **the DB was never modified** and the service recovered anyway. The 9,800 ms waits were to *acquire a pooled connection*, upstream of the DB — not query time. |
| **Pod flapping / LB ejection** | **Symptom, not cause** | Readiness failures began at 14:08, *after* pool saturation at 13:58. Pods went unhealthy because the request queue backed up behind the saturated pool. Correctly parked by oncall at 14:21. |
| **`async_receipts` flag (also in `v2.31.0`)** | **Not implicated** | It moves receipt emission *off* the request path onto a background worker — if anything it *shortens* connection hold time. No evidence of impact. Noted only to prevent re-litigation. |
